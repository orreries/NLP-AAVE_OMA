About this project:

Hi, I'm Oriel and this project is interrogating the usage of AAVE within popular media. It does so using NLP Semantic Similarity techniques.

Using Sentence Transformers it implements the all-MiniLM-L12-v2 model to calculate cosine similarity between the comedic movie scripts and a corpus of Black Twitter posts.

Reflections & Findings

Reflection

I first applied an n-gram dictionary matching procedure to see if there was a demonstrable amount of AAVE usage in the selected films. Using a cleaned AAVE glossary, each script was evaluated for single-word and multi-word AAVE expressions. Next Friday was used as the control, as it is widely considered Black media. Next Friday contained more identifiable AAVE terms than movies such as Bridesmaids, Superbad, or The Hangover; however, it was still present in non-Black films.

This step confirmed that AAVE is used in some scripts written from a non-Black perspective. Still, I was able to reach this conclusion based on my familiarity with AAVE, and it may be more challenging and intensive for less familiar researchers to get the same conclusion.

Semantic Similarity Results (Cosine Similarity)

Across all films, the semantic similarity scores cluster tightly together, regardless of whether the film uses noticeable AAVE features. Some cursory clustering shows:

High Similarity Cluster (~0.20–0.23)

  • Next Friday — 0.2208
  • Bridesmaids — 0.2217
  • The Hangover — 0.2339
  • Superbad — 0.2013
  • Hot Tub Time Machine — 0.2040

Mid/Low Similarity Cluster

  • Pineapple Express — 0.1743
  • Ted — 0.1471
  • Tropic Thunder — 0.1872

Outlier Low Similarity

  • Harold & Kumar Go to White Castle — 0.0892 (least similar overall)

What is striking is that the film with the most AAVE lexical content (Next Friday) has nearly the same semantic similarity score as films with comparatively little AAVE usage. This pattern also extends to the mean similarities, max similarities, and similarity distributions. These metrics also remain tightly clustered across all nine films, despite dramatic differences in cultural grounding, dialogue style, register, and character demographics.

Why the Model Behaves This Way

The SentenceTransformer MiniLM model primarily encodes:

  • topical similarity (conflict, relationships, humor, family, drama)
  • emotional tone
  • discourse structure

What it does not encode well includes:

  • vernacular features (AAVE syntax, habitual "be," copula absence, negative concord)
  • racialized linguistic identity
  • code-switching
  • cultural context
  • sociolinguistic register

As a result, even when AAVE-heavy tweets contain distinctly Black cultural and linguistic markers, the model maps them into a generic "informal comedic dialogue" space that many movies occupy— regardless of cultural specificity.

Consequently:

  • Next Friday and Bridesmaids appear almost identical to the model (~0.22 cosine).
  • Films with different racial and cultural grounding (Hot Tub Time Machine, Superbad, The Hangover) still fall in the same cluster.
  • More unusual films (Harold & Kumar, Scott Pilgrim) appear lower only because they diverge in topic, not linguistic style.

Implications: Structural Erasure of AAVE in Machine Learning Models

  1. AAVE appears invisible in semantic embedding space. Even when a script contains unmistakable AAVE terms and Black cultural rhetoric, the model collapses those linguistic features into generic English structures.
  2. Topic overrides dialect. The embeddings capture what is being discussed, not how it is expressed. Movies about interpersonal chaos, family conflict, and humor score similarly— even if the language used is culturally specific.
  3. AAVE is underrepresented in training data. MiniLM was trained on standard and majority-coded English corpora. As a result, AAVE patterns are treated as statistical noise rather than meaningful signals.
  4. Dialectal and cultural distinctions collapse into a single semantic space. From the model's perspective, all these movies are just "comedy scripts about messy people," regardless of race, culture, or vernacular.
  5. This extends beyond AAVE. Other minoritized Englishes (Chicano English, Hawaiian Pidgin, Cajun English, Caribbean English, Appalachian English) would similarly be flattened.

What These Results Demonstrate

General-purpose NLP models can detect thematic similarity across texts but fail to represent or distinguish the linguistic, cultural, and racialized nuances encoded in AAVE. AAVE is "standardized away" when vectorized. Machine learning systems reproduce existing linguistic hierarchies by not accounting for diverse English dialects. Without explicit dialect-aware training corpora, models will continue to erase the linguistic complexities of marginalized communities.
